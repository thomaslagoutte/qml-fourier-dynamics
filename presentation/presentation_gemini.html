Motivation — Learning Quantum Dynamics from Classical Data

Supervised task. Given i.i.d. samples $\mathcal D_T=\{(\boldsymbol{x}_t,y_t)\}_{t=1}^{T}\sim\mathcal{D}^{T}$ with labels $y_t = f_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}(\boldsymbol{x}_t)$, where $$f_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}(\boldsymbol{x})\;=\;\mel{\psi(\boldsymbol{x},\boldsymbol{\alpha})}{O}{\psi(\boldsymbol{x},\boldsymbol{\alpha})}, \qquad \ket{\psi(\boldsymbol{x},\boldsymbol{\alpha})}=e^{\,i\,H(\boldsymbol{x},\boldsymbol{\alpha})\,t}\ket{\psi_0}$$ return $h$ that $\varepsilon$-approximates $f_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}$ on unseen $\boldsymbol{x}\sim\mathcal{D}$ (PAC sense, formalised next slide).

Two faces of the same concept class

  • Hamiltonian dynamics (physical): $f_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}(\boldsymbol{x})=\mel{\psi(\boldsymbol{x},\boldsymbol{\alpha})}{O}{\psi(\boldsymbol{x},\boldsymbol{\alpha})}$.
  • PQC functions (algorithmic): $h_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}(\boldsymbol{x})=\mel{0}{U^{\dagger}(\boldsymbol{x},\boldsymbol{\alpha})\,O\,U(\boldsymbol{x},\boldsymbol{\alpha})}{0}$.
  • Bridged by Hamiltonian simulation (Trotter, qubitization, LCU).

Knowns vs. unknowns

  • $\boldsymbol{x}$: per‑sample data (e.g. lattice mask $x_{ij}\!\in\!\{0,1\}$).
  • $\boldsymbol{\alpha}\!\in\![0,1]^{d}$: unknown, fixed global couplings (transverse field, mass, gauge coupling, ...).

Bridge between physical dynamics and PQC functions; PAC‑learnability transfers across it.
Adapted from Barthe et al. (2025).

Central question

Evaluating $f_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}(\boldsymbol{x})$ is generically $\mathsf{BQP}$‑hard $\Rightarrow$ a classical learner cannot even generate its own labels.
Can quantum access to the dynamics yield a provably efficient PAC learner where no classical algorithm can?

Probably Approximately Correct (PAC) Learning — definition

Originally introduced by L. Valiant (1984); we use the efficient, distribution‑independent formulation of Barthe et al. (Def. 1).

Definition 1 — Efficient PAC learnability (Barthe et al., 2025)

A concept class $\mathcal{C}=\{\mathcal{C}_n\}_n$ with $c:\mathcal X_n\to\mathcal Y_n\in\mathcal{C}_n$ is efficiently PAC‑learnable iff there exists an algorithm $\mathcal{A}$ such that,
$\forall\,n,\;\forall\,\varepsilon{>}0,\;\forall\,\delta{\in}(0,1],\; \forall\,c\in\mathcal{C}_n,\;\forall$ target distribution $\mathcal{D}$ on $\mathcal X_n$,
given an i.i.d. training dataset $\{(\boldsymbol{x}_t,c(\boldsymbol{x}_t))\}_{t=1}^{T}\sim\mathcal{D}^{T}$ of size $T\in\mathrm{poly}(n,\varepsilon^{-1},\delta^{-1})$, $\mathcal{A}$ returns a hypothesis $h=\mathcal{A}(T,\varepsilon,\delta)$ in time $\mathrm{poly}(n,\varepsilon^{-1},\delta^{-1})$ satisfying $$ \Pr_{\boldsymbol{x}\sim\mathcal{D}}\!\bigl[\, \lvert h(\boldsymbol{x})-c(\boldsymbol{x})\rvert\le\varepsilon \,\bigr]\;\ge\;1-\delta. $$

Reading guide

Approximately = error $\le\varepsilon$; Probably = with confidence $\ge 1-\delta$;
Efficient = sample size and runtime polynomial in $(n,\varepsilon^{-1},\delta^{-1})$;
Distribution‑independent = the bound must hold for any $\mathcal{D}$.
$\Longrightarrow$ a much stronger requirement than typical statistical‑learning average‑case guarantees.

PAC Learning — quantum vs. classical, separation target

PAC learnability is parameterized by the model of computation granted to the learner; everything else (samples, distribution, guarantees) is identical.

Classical‑efficient PAC

$\mathcal{A}$ and $h$ are uniform classical poly‑time TMs (or non‑uniform: belong to $\mathsf{P/poly}$).

Lower bounds typically come from cryptographic or complexity‑theoretic hardness.

Quantum‑efficient PAC

$\mathcal{A}$ is a uniform quantum poly‑time circuit; the hypothesis $h$ may be evaluated by a quantum sub‑routine on a fresh $\boldsymbol{x}$.

Same PAC bound; the only relaxation is the hardware.

The labels are quantum either way. Both learners receive the same dataset $\{(\boldsymbol{x}_t,c(\boldsymbol{x}_t))\}_t$; generating $c(\boldsymbol{x}_t)$ for the concept classes we will consider already requires $\mathsf{BQP}$ power.

Target separation (Barthe et al., Thm. 2 & Thm. 3)

Exhibit a family $\mathcal{C}$ that is efficiently quantum PAC‑learnable and, under the standard complexity hypothesis $\mathsf{BQP}\not\subseteq\mathsf{P/poly}$, not efficiently classically PAC‑learnable.
$\Rightarrow$ provable, super‑polynomial quantum‑classical learning advantage on a natural, physically motivated task.

Pauli‑encoded PQCs — state‑level Fourier structure

Definition 2 — Pauli encoding (Barthe et al., 2025)

A Pauli‑encoded circuit on $n$ qubits is a unitary $U:\boldsymbol{\alpha}\in[0,1]^{d}\to U(2^{n})$ composed of $N_f\in\mathrm{poly}(n)$ fixed gates and $L\in\mathrm{poly}(n)$ parametrized gates $$ V_l(\boldsymbol{\alpha})\;:=\;e^{\,i\pi\,P_l\,\alpha_{i_l}}, \qquad P_l\in\{I,X,Y,Z\} \otimes n,\;\; i_l\in\{1,\dots,d\}. $$ The same parameter $\alpha_{i_l}$ may be re‑uploaded (used in several $V_l$'s) — essential for Trotterized evolutions.

Lemma 1 — Fourier representation of $U(\boldsymbol{\alpha})\ket{0}$ (Schuld–Sweke–Meyer, 2021)

Any Pauli‑encoded circuit produces a state that is a finite trigonometric polynomial in $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$: $$ \ket{\phi(\boldsymbol{\alpha})}\;=\;U(\boldsymbol{\alpha})\ket{0}\;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{2^{n}}\;\sum_{\vec l\in[-L,+L]^{d}}\! a_{\vec l,k}\,e^{\,i\pi\,\boldsymbol{\alpha}\cdot\vec l}\,\ket{k}, \qquad a_{\vec l,k}\in\mathbb{C}. $$ Unknowns $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$ appear only as global phases; the amplitudes $a_{\vec l,k}$ are $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$‑independent structural data.

$\Rightarrow$ every Trotter / qubitization / LCU realisation of $e^{iH(\boldsymbol{x},\boldsymbol{\alpha})t}$ belongs to Definition 2; the theory will transfer to physical Hamiltonian dynamics in Sec. V.

Pauli‑encoded PQCs — expectation values as Fourier series

Corollary 1 — PQC function (Eqs. (4)–(8) of Barthe et al.)

For a Pauli observable $P$, the PQC function $$ f(\boldsymbol{\alpha})\;=\;\mel{0}{U^{\dagger}(\boldsymbol{\alpha})\,P\,U(\boldsymbol{\alpha})}{0} \;=\; \sum_{\vec l\in\mathcal{L}}\,b_{\vec l}\,e^{\,i\pi\,\boldsymbol{\alpha}\cdot\vec l}, \qquad \mathcal{L}:=[-2L,+2L]^{d}, $$ is a finite trigonometric polynomial with at most $m:=(4L+1)^{d}$ frequencies, coefficients $\boldsymbol{b}=(b_{\vec l})_{\vec l\in\mathcal{L}}\in\mathbb{C}^{m}$.

Lattice doubling $L\!\to\!2L$

Plug Lemma 1 into $\mel{\phi(\boldsymbol{\alpha})}{P}{\phi(\boldsymbol{\alpha})}$: $$ f(\boldsymbol{\alpha})=\!\!\sum_{k,k',\vec l,\vec l'}\! \overline{a_{\vec l,k}}\,a_{\vec l',k'}\,P_{kk'}\, e^{\,i\pi\boldsymbol{\alpha}\cdot(\vec l'-\vec l)}. $$ Group terms by $\vec l:=\vec l'-\vec l$:
$\;\vec l\in[-L,L]^{d}-[-L,L]^{d}=\mathcal{L}$,
$\,b_{\vec l}=\sum_{k,k'}\sum_{\vec l'-\vec l''=\vec l} \overline{a_{\vec l'',k}}\,a_{\vec l',k'}\,P_{kk'}$.

Structural takeaways

  • Finite integer lattice $\mathcal{L}=[-2L,2L]^{d}$, $\vert \mathcal{L}\vert =(4L+1)^{d}$.
  • Unknowns appear only in phases $e^{i\pi\boldsymbol{\alpha}\cdot\vec l}$: structure and parameters decouple.
  • Learning $f$ $\Leftrightarrow$ learning $\boldsymbol{b}\in\mathbb{C}^{m}$ $\Rightarrow$ linear regression once $\boldsymbol{b}$ is exposed.

The PQC Concept Class — Definition 3

Definition 3 — PQC-based functions concept class (Barthe et al.)

Let $U(\boldsymbol{x},\boldsymbol{\alpha})$ be a Pauli-encoded circuit on $n$ qubits in which $\boldsymbol{x}\in\{0,1\}^{N}$ bit-encodes the fixed sub-circuit (lattice mask, geometry, ...) and $\boldsymbol{\alpha}\in[0,1]^{d_{n}}$, $d_{n}=f(n)$, are the unknown shared parameters. For an observable $O$, $$ \mathcal{C}^{\mathsf{PQC}}_{n,f}\;=\; \Bigl\{\,\boldsymbol{x}\,\mapsto\,\mel{0}{U^{\dagger}(\boldsymbol{x},\boldsymbol{\alpha})\,O\,U(\boldsymbol{x},\boldsymbol{\alpha})}{0} \;:\;\boldsymbol{\alpha}\in[0,1]^{d_{n}}\Bigr\}. $$ Two distinct scaling regimes: $f(n)=\log n\,\Rightarrow\,\mathcal{C}^{\mathsf{PQC}}_{\log}$ (PAC-learnable); $\quad f(n)=\mathrm{poly}(n)\,\Rightarrow\,\mathcal{C}^{\mathsf{PQC}}_{\mathrm{poly}}$ (Computationally hard).

Core Identity (Eq. 8 with $x$ tracked)

Sandwich $O$ and group by frequency as on Slide 06, explicitly tracking the $\boldsymbol{x}$-dependence of the amplitudes $a_{\vec l,k}(\boldsymbol{x})$: $$ f_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}(\boldsymbol{x})\;=\; \sum_{\vec l\in\mathcal{L}}\;b_{\vec l}(\boldsymbol{x})\; e^{\,i\pi\,\boldsymbol{\alpha}\cdot\vec l}, \qquad \mathcal{L}=[-2L,+2L]^{d_{n}},\;|\mathcal{L}|=m. $$ Here, $\boldsymbol{b}(\boldsymbol{x})=(b_{\vec l}(\boldsymbol{x}))_{\vec l\in\mathcal{L}}\in\mathbb{C}^{m}$ is an $\boldsymbol{x}$-dependent feature vector; the frequency phases remain entirely $\boldsymbol{x}$-independent.

$\Rightarrow$ Strategic Consequence: The PAC dataset $\{(\boldsymbol{x}_t,f_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}(\boldsymbol{x}_t))\}$ inherently maps to the structure of a generalized linear model within the latent feature space $\boldsymbol{b}(\boldsymbol{x}_t)$.

Learning Reduction — Features and Phases Decouple

The core identity is a complex linear model across two dual feature maps — one strictly extracted from the input data, and one carrying the unknown parameter values.

Bilinear Factorization

$$ f_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}(\boldsymbol{x})\;=\;\boldsymbol{b}(\boldsymbol{x})\,\cdot\,\boldsymbol{w}(\boldsymbol{\alpha}), \qquad \boldsymbol{w}(\boldsymbol{\alpha})=\bigl(e^{\,i\pi\,\vec l\cdot\boldsymbol{\alpha}}\bigr)_{\vec l\in\mathcal{L}}\! \in\mathbb{C}^{m},\;\; \boldsymbol{b}(\boldsymbol{x})\in\mathbb{C}^{m}. $$
$\boldsymbol{b}(\boldsymbol{x})$ :
Known per-sample feature vector, explicitly computable by Algorithm $\mathcal{A}$ (Sec. III).
$\boldsymbol{w}(\boldsymbol{\alpha})$ :
Unknown but perfectly $\boldsymbol{x}$-independent. It lies constrained on the $m$-torus $\bigl\{\,e^{i\pi\vec l\cdot\boldsymbol{\alpha}}\,\bigr\}_{\vec l}$, strictly satisfying $\|\boldsymbol{w}\|_\infty=1$ and $\|\boldsymbol{w}\|_1\le m$.

Quantum–Classical Pipeline

  1. Quantum, per sample: Run Algorithm $\mathcal{A}$ on $U(\boldsymbol{x}_t,\cdot)$ via Hadamard tests to retrieve the empirical feature vector $\hat{\boldsymbol{b}}(\boldsymbol{x}_t)$ up to additive error $\varepsilon_b$.
  2. Classical, once: Execute the regularized linear regression: $$ \hat{\boldsymbol{w}}\,=\,\arg\min_{\|\boldsymbol{w}\|_1\le\Lambda_1} \bigl\|\,\boldsymbol{y}-\hat B\,\boldsymbol{w}\bigr\|_2^{2}, \;\;\hat B_{t,\vec l}=\hat b_{\vec l}(\boldsymbol{x}_t). $$
  3. Inference: $h(\boldsymbol{x})=\hat{\boldsymbol{b}}(\boldsymbol{x})\cdot\hat{\boldsymbol{w}}$.

Why this reduction is the whole game

  • Complete Elimination: The classical regressor never touches or fits the physical couplings $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$.
  • Sample Complexity: Controlled entirely by the $\ell_1$-norm bound $\|\boldsymbol{w}\|_1$ (via LASSO, Theorem 6), and critically not by the exponential ambient dimension $|\mathcal{L}|$.
  • The New Frontier: The hard part is now purely quantum—efficiently extracting $\boldsymbol{b}(\boldsymbol{x})$ via Algorithm $\mathcal{A}$.

Algorithm $A$ — Architecture and Register Design

Objective (Theorem 1, Sec. III of Barthe et al.)

Compile any Pauli-encoded $U(\boldsymbol{x},\boldsymbol{\alpha})$ into a parameter-free circuit $A(U)$ on a larger Hilbert space such that $$ A(U)\,\ket{0}_{f}\ket{0}_{a}\ket{0}_{c}\;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{2^{n}}\;\sum_{\vec l\in[-L,L]^{d}}\! a_{\vec l,k}(\boldsymbol{x})\; \ket{\vec l}_{f}\,\ket{0}_{a}\,\ket{k}_{c}. $$ Each Fourier index $\vec l$ is now a physical, addressable basis state — we can post-select or Hadamard-test on it.

Three registers $\bigl[\,n_T=n+1+\sum_s n_s\,\bigr]$

$c$: circuit register, $n$ qubits.

$a$: parity ancilla, 1 qubit.

$f=f_1\!\cdots f_d$: frequency register; $n_s=1+\lceil 2\log s\rceil$ qubits per dimension, with circular-boundary increment / decrement gates $V_+,V_-=V_+^{\dagger}$.

Resource counts (Theorem 1). Total $n'=n+d\lceil\log L\rceil+1$ qubits, $L'=N_f+L(2n+d\lceil\log L\rceil)$ gates; all $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$-independent.

Left: a 2-qubit Pauli-encoded $U(\boldsymbol{x})$. Right: the compiled $A(U)$. The $V_\pm$ blocks per encoding gate stand in for the $D{\cdot}G{\cdot}D$ construction (next slide).

Algorithm $A$ — the $D \cdot G \cdot D$ transformation

Setup. WLOG every encoding gate is $V_l(\boldsymbol{\alpha})=\exp\!\bigl(i\pi\alpha_{s_l}P_l\bigr)$ with $P_l=\prod_{k:b_{l,k}=1}Z_k$ (any $X,Y$ gates are absorbed into the surrounding fixed gates). Algorithm $A$ replaces it with a completely decoupled construction:

$$V_l(\boldsymbol{\alpha})\;\;\longmapsto\;\; D(b_l)\cdot G(s_l)\cdot D(b_l)$$
$D(b_l)$
CNOT cascade $\prod_{k:b_{l,k}=1}\!\mathrm{CNOT}^{(c_k\to a)}$, writes the parity $\,p_l(k)=\bigoplus_{j:b_{l,j}=1}\!k_j\in\{0,1\}$ onto $a$.
$G(s_l)$
parity-controlled increment / decrement of $f_{s_l}$: apply $V_+$ when $p_l=1$, apply $V_-$ when $p_l=0$.
$D(b_l)$
second CNOT cascade uncomputes $a$ back to $\ket{0}$ (since $D^{2}=I$), making the ancilla reusable for the next gate.

Net effect

The physical phase $e^{i\pi\alpha_{s_l}\sigma_l(k)}$ of $V_l(\boldsymbol{\alpha})$ becomes a discrete additive shift of the auxiliary register $f_{s_l}$: $$ \ket{l}_f\ket{0}_a\ket{k}_c\;\longmapsto\; \ket{l+e_{s_l}\sigma_l(k)}_f\ket{0}_a\ket{k}_c,\quad \sigma_l=(-1)^{1\oplus p_l}. $$ Accumulated over the $L$ parameterized gates, this builds out the frequency grid $\vec l$ defined in Lemma 1.

$D(b_l) \cdot G(s_l) \cdot D(b_l)$

One conceptual block; assuming two relevant $c$-qubits where $b_{l,k_1}\!=\!b_{l,k_2}\!=\!1$. The two $D$ cascades compute the parity, sandwich $G$, and reset the ancilla $a$.

Algorithm $A$ — Correctness by Induction (Theorem 1)

Theorem 1 (Barthe et al., Sec. III)

For every Pauli-encoded $U$, the parameter-free circuit $A(U)$ prepares $$ \ket{\phi'}\;=\;A(U)\ket{0}\;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{2^n}\;\sum_{\vec l\in[-L,L]^d}\!a_{\vec l,k}(\boldsymbol{x})\, \ket{\vec l}_{f}\ket{0}_{a}\ket{k}_{c} $$ in $\mathrm{poly}(n,d)$ qubits/gates, with no knowledge of $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$. Equivalently, $A$ physically tags the Fourier indices of Lemma 1.

Proof. Induction on the number of processed gates, with the invariant $\;\ket{\phi'_t}=\sum_{k,\vec l}\,a^{(t)}_{\vec l,k}\,\ket{\vec l}_f\ket{0}_a\ket{k}_c$, where $a^{(t)}_{\vec l,k}$ are the exact Lemma 1 amplitudes of the truncated circuit $U_{\le t}$.

1. Base Case

$A(I)\ket{0}=\ket{0}_f\ket{0}_a\ket{0}_c$, which perfectly matches Lemma 1 for the trivial circuit $U=I$. ✓

2. Fixed gate $U^{(c)}$

Lifted as $I^{(f)}\!\otimes\!I^{(a)}\!\otimes\!U^{(c)}$. Therefore $a^{(t+1)}_{\vec l,k}=\sum_{k'}\!U_{kk'}a^{(t)}_{\vec l,k'}$, leaving frequencies completely untouched. ✓

3. Encoding gate

$D\!\cdot\!G\!\cdot\!D$ systematically maps $a^{(t)}_{\vec l,k}\!\to\!a^{(t+1)}_{\vec l+e_{s_l}\sigma_l(k),\,k}$, flawlessly matching the phase $e^{i\pi\alpha_{s_l}P_l}$ on $\sum a^{(t)}\!e^{i\pi\boldsymbol{\alpha}\cdot\vec l}\ket k$. ✓

Why this matters

$A$ is deterministic and universal (across any Pauli-encoded $U$) and amplitude-encodes Lemma 1's spectrum with only polynomial overhead. Sandwiching $A$ into a standard Hadamard test on $\ket{\vec l}_{f}$ directly yields each coefficient $b_{\vec l}(\boldsymbol{x})$ up to additive error (Corollaries 1 & 2, next slide).

Amplitude-Encoding the Coefficients (Corollary 1)

Theorem 1 lifts a circuit to its state-level Fourier expansion. Sandwiching $A(U)$ around an observable $P$ lifts that further to the expectation-value Fourier expansion — establishing a direct amplitude encoding of $\boldsymbol{b}(\boldsymbol{x})$ directly onto the frequency register.

Corollary 1 — Expectation-value Fourier state (Eq. 9)

Defining the compound circuit $\mathcal{A}(U,P)\mathrel{:=}\bigl(\bra{0}_a\bra{0}_c\bigr)\,\mathcal{A}(U)^{\dagger}\,P\,\mathcal{A}(U)$, $$ \mathcal{A}(U,P)\;\ket{0}_f\ket{0}_a\ket{0}_c \;=\; \frac{1}{\|\boldsymbol{b}(\boldsymbol{x})\|_2} \!\sum_{\vec l\in\mathcal{L}}\! b_{\vec l}(\boldsymbol{x})\; \ket{\vec l}_f \;+\; \ket{\dots}_f\,\ket{0_\perp}_{ac}. $$ Post-selection on $\ket{0}_a\!\ket{0}_c$ succeeds with probability $p^\star=\|\boldsymbol{b}(\boldsymbol{x})\|_2^{2}$; the surviving $f$-register strictly amplitude-encodes the Fourier coefficients of $f_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}(\boldsymbol{x}) =\bra{0}U^{\dagger}(\boldsymbol{x},\boldsymbol{\alpha})\,P\,U(\boldsymbol{x},\boldsymbol{\alpha})\ket{0}$.
